What is GBS?

A rare neurological disorder where the immune system attacks the peripheral nervous system — the nerves outside the brain and spinal cord.

Who Can Get It?

Anyone at any age — children, youth, elderly. More common in adults over 50 and in males. GBS is NOT contagious and NOT inherited.

The Pune 2025 Outbreak

In January 2025, a major GBS outbreak hit Pune — 215+ cases linked to contaminated drinking water. Outbreak declared over in March 2025.

Recovery is Possible

Most people with GBS fully recover. Timely hospitalisation and treatment — IVIg or Plasma Exchange — can shorten severity significantly.

Causes & Risk Factors

Understanding what triggers GBS helps with prevention and early detection.

How Does GBS Happen?

  • GBS is an autoimmune condition — the immune system mistakenly attacks the body's own peripheral nerves.
  • The exact cause is not fully understood, but most cases follow a bacterial or viral infection.
  • About 2 in every 3 people had diarrhoea or a respiratory illness before developing GBS.

Most Common Triggers

Most Common

Campylobacter jejuni (bacteria)

Most common GBS trigger. Causes gastroenteritis (diarrhoea, nausea, vomiting). About 1 in every 1,000 people with this infection gets GBS. Found in contaminated water and undercooked poultry. Primary cause in the Pune 2025 outbreak.

Influenza (flu) virus

A recognised viral trigger for GBS.

Cytomegalovirus, Epstein-Barr, Zika, SARS-CoV-2

Other viral infections linked to GBS development.

Surgery

Occasionally triggers GBS.

Very Rare Risk

Vaccines

Risk is extremely low: 1-2 extra cases per million flu vaccine doses. Far less than the risk of getting GBS from flu infection itself.

The Pune 2025 outbreak was linked to Campylobacter jejuni and Norovirus detected in patient samples and the water supply. Contaminated drinking water was identified as the primary cause.

Precautions & Prevention

Prevention focuses on avoiding the infections that trigger GBS, especially through food and water safety.

Water Safety Highest Priority

  • Always drink treated, filtered, or boiled water — especially during outbreak alerts.
  • If boiling: boil for at least 20 minutes, then let it cool before drinking. Do not change the container.
  • Avoid drinking water from unknown or untreated sources — wells, tankers, and local supply points of unclear treatment.
  • Regularly maintain and test water purification systems at home or work/school/public places.

Food Hygiene (School, Office, Home, Travel)

  • Wash hands thoroughly with soap before eating and after using the toilet.
  • Avoid raw or improperly cooked street food during outbreak periods.
  • Wash all fruits and vegetables with clean, treated water before eating.
  • Refrigerate food properly; do not eat food left out at room temperature for hours.

Infection Prevention

  • Wash hands regularly especially after travel, using public transport, or visiting crowded spaces.
  • Get vaccinated against flu annually.
  • If you develop diarrhoea or a respiratory illness, consult a doctor promptly. Watch for neurological symptoms in the days and weeks that follow.
  • Stay informed about local health advisories and water quality alerts from municipal authorities.

Travel & Public Spaces

  • Follow standard hygiene precautions during travel — safe water, clean food, regular handwashing.
  • GBS is not contagious — no travel restrictions are needed during an outbreak.
  • Carry a bottle of safe, treated water when traveling or commuting.
  • During outbreaks: follow government water safety advisories; avoid street-side drinking water.

For Parents — Watch Your Children

  • Children with GBS may refuse to walk or complain of unusual leg pain — take this seriously and consult a doctor the same day.
  • Early symptoms in children often include pain and tingling sensation in hands and legs before visible weakness develops.
  • Ensure school canteens and home water sources meet safety standards.
  • Educate children about handwashing hygiene from an early age.

Treatment & Recovery

There is currently no cure for GBS, but two effective treatments can significantly reduce severity and shorten recovery time.

Immediate Action Required: Anyone suspected of having GBS must be hospitalised immediately. Do not attempt to manage symptoms at home. Every hour of delay increases risk.

Treatment Options

Treatment 1

IVIg — Intravenous Immunoglobulin Therapy

  • Injections of healthy proteins (immunoglobulins) from donated blood.
  • Dilutes and neutralises the harmful antibodies attacking your nerves.
  • Shortens recovery time significantly.
Cost note (India): Approx. Rs. 20,000 per adult dose — a significant expense for families.
Treatment 2

Plasma Exchange (PE) — Plasmapheresis

  • Blood is removed; plasma (the liquid part containing bad antibodies) is separated out.
  • Blood cells are returned to the body with replacement fluid.
  • Removes the antibodies that are damaging the nerves.
Both treatments are equally effective if started within 2 weeks of symptom onset.
Important: Anti-inflammatory steroid hormones called corticosteroids have been tried to reduce severity of GBS, but clinical trials have shown this treatment is NOT effective. Do not self-medicate with steroids.

Possible Complications (If Untreated or Delayed)

Respiratory failure from breathing muscle paralysis
Blood infection (sepsis)
Lung blood clots
Cardiac arrest
Long-term permanent paralysis or nerve damage

Myth Buster

All myths and misconceptions about GBS — corrected with evidence-based facts. Click a myth to reveal the truth.

MYTH "GBS is contagious — stay away from people who have it." ▼
FACT

GBS is NOT contagious. You cannot catch it from another person. No isolation or travel restrictions are needed during a GBS outbreak.

MYTH "GBS is a death sentence — if you get it, you won't survive." ▼
FACT

Most people fully recover from GBS, even from the most severe cases. With timely treatment and intensive care, even those requiring ventilator support usually survive.

MYTH "GBS only affects old people." ▼
FACT

GBS affects people of all ages including children and youth. In the Pune 2025 outbreak, over 55% of cases were under 30 years old.

MYTH "Vaccines cause GBS — avoid all vaccinations." ▼
FACT

The risk is extremely small (1-2 extra cases per million flu vaccine doses). You are far more likely to get GBS from the flu infection itself than from the flu vaccine. Vaccine benefits far outweigh this tiny risk.

MYTH "GBS is inherited — if a parent had it, children will get it too." ▼
FACT

GBS is not a hereditary genetic disease. There is no confirmed gene that directly causes GBS. Some genes may influence immune response, but GBS cannot be "passed down" to children.

MYTH "If I had diarrhoea and recovered, I cannot get GBS." ▼
FACT

GBS can develop days to weeks after a diarrheal or respiratory illness, even after the infection seems to have resolved. Watch for new neurological symptoms like tingling or progressive weakness in the weeks following any infection.

MYTH "Steroids will cure or help GBS." ▼
FACT

Corticosteroids have been tested in clinical trials and proven ineffective for GBS. The correct treatments are IVIg (intravenous immunoglobulin) and plasma exchange.

MYTH "GBS outbreaks are normal and happen everywhere regularly." ▼
FACT

GBS outbreaks are very rare. The Pune 2025 outbreak was highly unusual — driven by contaminated drinking water in a localised urban area. Rapid water sanitation measures successfully contained it within weeks.

Do's & Don'ts + FAQs

A quick reference guide for the public — what to do, what to avoid, and answers to common questions.

Do's

  • Drink filtered, boiled, or treated water always.
  • Wash hands with soap before eating and after using the toilet.
  • Cook poultry and meat thoroughly before eating.
  • Get annual flu vaccination.
  • Seek a doctor if diarrhoea or respiratory illness is followed by leg weakness or tingling.
  • Go to hospital immediately if neurological symptoms spread.
  • Tell your doctor about recent infections or vaccinations.
  • Follow government water and food safety advisories.
  • Pursue physical therapy and rehabilitation during recovery.
  • Seek mental health support — recovery from GBS is emotionally difficult.

Don'ts

  • Don't ignore progressive weakness or tingling in limbs.
  • Don't try to manage GBS symptoms at home — seek medical care.
  • Don't delay treatment — every hour matters.
  • Don't take steroids as a GBS treatment — clinical trials show they do not work.
  • Don't drink water from unknown or untreated sources.
  • Don't eat undercooked chicken or unverified street food during outbreak alerts.
  • Don't avoid all vaccines out of GBS fear — flu infection carries far higher risk.
  • Don't assume GBS is contagious — no isolation from patients is needed.
  • Don't ignore a child who refuses to walk or complains of unexplained leg pain.
  • Don't skip mental health support during the recovery process.

FAQs — Common Questions from Parents & Youth

Q1 Can my child get GBS? ▼

Yes. GBS can affect anyone, including children and young adults. In the 2025 Pune outbreak, more than 55% of cases were under 30 years of age. Children may show early signs by refusing to walk or complaining of unexplained leg pain.

Q2 Is GBS the same as paralysis from a spinal injury? ▼

No. GBS-related weakness comes from nerve damage caused by an immune response — not from a spinal injury. Unlike a spinal injury, GBS nerve damage can heal. Most people with GBS recover fully with appropriate treatment.

Q3 Should I avoid the flu vaccine because of GBS risk? ▼

No. The risk of developing GBS from the flu vaccine is estimated at 1-2 extra cases per million doses (an extremely small risk). Studies consistently show that the risk of getting GBS from the flu infection itself is significantly higher than from the vaccine. Annual flu vaccination remains strongly recommended.

Q4 Can GBS be prevented completely? ▼

Maintaining good hygiene, ensuring safe drinking water, properly cooking food, and getting vaccinated against flu significantly reduce your risk of the infections that can trigger GBS.

Q5 What tests confirm GBS? ▼

Doctors assess symptoms and perform a physical examination checking reflexes (which are reduced or absent in GBS). Additional tests may include nerve conduction velocity studies, cerebrospinal fluid analysis, and MRI scans. Importantly, treatment should not be delayed while waiting for test results — GBS diagnosis can be made based on clinical signs.
